# Conversation Archetypes

Synthesized from 2,800+ successful sales calls across 28 batch reports. These are the 5 distinct conversation patterns that account for the vast majority of Claim Warriors intake and follow-up calls.

---

## Archetype 1: The Frustrated Fighter

**Frequency**: ~35% of calls
**Profile**: Customer has been battling their insurance company for weeks or months. They've received a lowball offer or outright denial. They're angry, exhausted, and ready to give up or escalate.
**Typical claim status**: Underpaid or Denied
**Initial tone**: Frustrated, sometimes angry

**How the call flows**:
1. Customer vents about insurance company behavior — delays, lowball offers, runaround, unanswered calls
2. Rep validates frustration: "Yeah, they do that to everyone. That's how they make money."
3. Rep educates on insurance tactics: explains why the insurer is doing what they're doing
4. Rep presents CW as the weapon the customer didn't know they had
5. Customer shifts from frustrated to hopeful once they understand CW's leverage (licensing, appraisal, negotiation)
6. Close: Direct Ask — "I just need your information to send the contract"

**Key turning point**: The moment the rep explains *why* the insurance company is behaving this way and that CW has legal standing to fight back. The customer goes from feeling powerless to feeling like they have an advocate.

**Real example flow**:
> Customer: "Progressive is kinda giving me the runaround with my claim, so."
> Rep: "So we're a public adjuster. We help people with their home insurance claims. How's it going with yours?"
> [Customer explains, rep validates]
> Rep: "They treat us differently because we have a license. So the state gives us a license to represent people with their insurance claims. They're kind of scared of us."
> Customer shifts from frustrated → hopeful → committed

## Archetype 2: The Confused First-Timer

**Frequency**: ~25% of calls
**Profile**: Customer has never dealt with insurance claims before. They don't understand the process, terminology, or their rights. They found CW through a website search or referral and aren't sure what a public adjuster even does.
**Typical claim status**: New or Underpaid
**Initial tone**: Confused, cooperative

**How the call flows**:
1. Customer asks basic questions: "What do you guys do?" / "How does this work?"
2. Rep explains public adjusting in plain language: "Similar to a lawyer, but for insurance claims"
3. Rep asks about the damage — identifies claim type and starts qualification
4. Customer reveals they have damage but don't know what their insurance should be paying
5. Rep educates on what the customer is entitled to — often much more than they realized
6. Rep offers to handle everything: "You don't have to talk to anyone anymore"
7. Close: Assumptive — "I'm going to send you a document now"

**Key turning point**: When the rep quantifies the potential value. "Your contractor quoted $36,000 but we usually negotiate 2-3x that amount." The customer's eyes open to how much they've been leaving on the table.

**Real example flow**:
> Customer: "I submitted my claim through AllState, and they sent back an estimate of $2,800."
> Rep: "How much is your contractor saying the repairs will cost?"
> Customer: "The young man said it's gonna be about $36,000 or better to fix it the way it's supposed to be."
> Rep: "We're gonna negotiate for double or triple the amount your contractor is quoting for."
> Customer shifts from confused → eager → committed

## Archetype 3: The Skeptical Shopper

**Frequency**: ~15% of calls
**Profile**: Customer has been burned before — by their insurer, by a prior adjuster, by a contractor, or by someone who promised results and didn't deliver. They're guarded and need proof before committing.
**Typical claim status**: Denied or Underpaid
**Initial tone**: Skeptical, cautious

**How the call flows**:
1. Customer asks pointed questions: "How do I know you're legit?" / "What's your BBB rating?" / "I already had an adjuster who screwed it up"
2. Rep doesn't get defensive — validates the skepticism: "I totally understand. Let me show you..."
3. Rep provides proof: license numbers, Google reviews, fee structure explanation, "no win no fee"
4. Customer tests with more objections — cost, timeline, guarantees
5. Rep handles each transparently: "If I don't think we can win, I'll let you know. I won't string you along."
6. Close: Trial → Direct Ask — "Do you have any questions?" then "Let me send the contract"

**Key turning point**: Transparency about what CW *can't* do is often more convincing than promises. "If we can't win you anything, we don't charge you anything" combined with "I'll tell you if I think it's a waste of time" builds trust.

**Real example flow**:
> Customer: "I'm asking you how come you guys aren't listed under the Better Business Bureau? We just don't wanna be scammed."
> Rep: "This is our license number. [provides it] That's our license to do public adjusting work in the state."
> Rep: "If we don't win, we don't charge you anything."
> Customer shifts from skeptical → cautious → committed

## Archetype 4: The Document Collector

**Frequency**: ~20% of calls
**Profile**: Customer has already signed a contract. This is a follow-up call to collect missing documents, photos, estimates, or policy information. The customer may be overwhelmed by what's being asked of them.
**Typical claim status**: In progress (already signed)
**Initial tone**: Cooperative but sometimes frustrated by paperwork

**How the call flows**:
1. Rep opens with specific context: "Thanks for sending the pictures. I just need a few more things."
2. Customer expresses difficulty: "I don't know how to do it" / "I'm computer illiterate" / "I don't have those receipts"
3. Rep simplifies: offers to handle it, provides workarounds, breaks it into small steps
4. Rep collects what they can on the call — name, email, policy number, claim number
5. Sets clear next steps: "Send me whatever you have, I'll sort it out on my end"
6. Close: Not a sales close — it's a task close. "I'll text you the email address. Send everything there."

**Key turning point**: When the rep takes the burden off the customer. "You can just ignore the survey for now. One of my colleagues will call you and we'll help you with it." The customer goes from overwhelmed to relieved.

**Real example flow**:
> Customer: "I'm 71, and I'm computer illiterate. So I have to depend on other people to do this for me."
> Rep: "Well, send it to me. I want you to send everything to me, and we will send what needs to be sent to them."
> Customer shifts from frustrated → relieved

## Archetype 5: The High-Value Negotiation Update

**Frequency**: ~5% of calls
**Profile**: Existing customer with a complex, high-value claim. The call is about strategy — appraisal decisions, attorney involvement, carrier negotiation tactics, or settlement discussions. These are the calls where the most money is at stake.
**Typical claim status**: Underpaid, in negotiation
**Initial tone**: Varies — anxious, eager, or frustrated depending on where the claim stands

**How the call flows**:
1. Rep provides update on claim status — what the carrier said, where the appraisal stands
2. Customer asks strategic questions: "Should we go to court?" / "How long will appraisal take?"
3. Rep explains options with pros/cons: appraisal vs. attorney vs. direct negotiation
4. Discussion of specific dollar amounts — CW estimate vs. carrier offer, potential recovery
5. Agreement on next steps and timeline
6. Close: Alignment close — "So here's what we're going to do this week..."

**Key turning point**: When the rep shows the customer the gap between what insurance offered and what CW's estimate shows. "They approved $45k. Our estimate is $233,000." The customer realizes the magnitude of what CW is fighting for.

**Real example flow**:
> Rep: "I'm actually seeing $233,000 here." [laughs]
> Customer: [had been told $22,000 by insurer]
> Rep: "William, final thing. If these guys don't agree to pay after you submit everything, I'm gonna trigger the appraisal clause of the policy and take the claim out of their hands."
> Customer shifts from anxious → confident → committed to strategy
