# Qualification Sequences by Claim Type

Extracted from 2,800+ successful calls. Each sequence follows the order top CW reps actually use — not a theoretical ideal, but what works in practice.

---

## Universal Intake Flow (All Claim Types)

Every intake call follows this general arc, regardless of claim type. Type-specific questions slot in at steps 3 and 5.

1. **Open**: Identify yourself, reference how the customer found CW (website, referral, callback)
2. **Initial Problem/Status**: "How's it been going with your insurance company so far?" or "Do you have a home insurance claim?"
3. **Damage Type & Cause**: "What kind of damage do you have?" / "What caused it?" → Branch to type-specific questions
4. **Current Payout / Estimate**: "How much did they offer you?" / "Do you have a contractor estimate?"
5. **Type-Specific Deep Dive**: Claim-type-specific qualification questions (see below)
6. **Information Collection**: Insurance company name, policy number, claim number, date of loss, property address, policyholder name(s), email
7. **Documentation Check**: "Do you have photos?" / "Do you have the denial letter?" / "Do you have contractor estimates?"
8. **Red Flag Check**: Mortgage status, prior adjusters, repairs already done, claim age
9. **Value Framing**: "We usually negotiate 2-3x what contractors quote" / "Our estimates are typically 150-300% higher"
10. **Close**: Send contract via DocuSign, walk through signing if needed

## Water Claims

**Distribution**: ~22% of all calls (most common identifiable type)
**Common statuses**: Underpaid (most common), Denied, New

### Type-Specific Qualification Questions

**Cause / Source** (ask first — determines everything):
- "What caused the water damage?"
- "Was it like a sudden pipe burst, or how did it happen?"
- "Where did the water come from — pipe, roof, appliance, sewer?"

**Why this matters**: Sudden events (pipe burst, appliance overflow) are typically covered. Slow leaks, "constant seepage," and gradual damage are frequently denied. The cause determines the entire strategy.

**Extent / Scope**:
- "How many rooms were affected?"
- "Is there mold?"
- "Has remediation started?"
- "How long did the water sit before it was mitigated?"

**Coverage Specifics**:
- "Do you have flood insurance from FEMA?" (critical — standard homeowner policies don't cover flood)
- "Is this a condo/apartment? Is there an HOA policy?"
- "Are you displaced? What are your living expenses?"
- "Did your water bill spike?" (counters "constant seepage" argument)

**Documentation**:
- "Do you have a plumber's report that says what caused the damage?"
- "Do you have photos before remediation started?"
- "Did the remediation company give you a scope of work?"

### Water-Specific Red Flags

| Signal | Good Sign | Bad Sign |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| Cause | Sudden pipe burst, appliance overflow | Slow leak, "constant seepage," gradual |
| Mold | Documented, within policy limits | Sole basis for claim, or exceeds mold cap |
| Timing | Reported immediately | Weeks/months before reporting |
| Multiple units | Can pursue multiple policies | Blame game between HOA and homeowner |
| Remediation | Not started, or well-documented | Done without photos, by insurance-referred company |

### Water-Specific Objections
- Insurance claims "constant seepage" or lack of maintenance
- Mold coverage caps ($10k-$25k typical)
- HOA policy deductibles and blame-shifting
- Mitigation company paid directly by insurer (leaving customer with nothing for actual repairs)

## Roof Claims

**Distribution**: ~12% of all calls
**Common statuses**: Underpaid, Denied (wear and tear)

### Type-Specific Qualification Questions

**Cause / Event**:
- "What was the cause of damage — wind, hail, storm, tree?"
- "When did the storm happen?"
- "Was there a declared weather event in your area?"

**Roof Details** (critical for strategy):
- "How old is your roof?"
- "Do you know what material the roof is — shingle, tile, metal?"
- "Are there missing shingles or visible damage?"
- "Has the insurance company's adjuster inspected yet?"

**Why roof age matters**: Older roofs (15+ years) can actually be *better* for the customer — if materials are obsolete/discontinued, the customer may be entitled to a full replacement rather than a patch.

**Scope of Damage**:
- "Is there any interior damage besides the roof — leaks, ceiling stains, wall cracks?"
- "Is the siding damaged too?"
- "Did the storm affect other structures — garage, fence, shed?"

**Prior Actions**:
- "Have you had a contractor come out for an estimate?"
- "Has anyone else inspected the roof — another adjuster, an engineer?"
- "Have you started any repairs?"

### Roof-Specific Red Flags

| Signal | Good Sign | Bad Sign |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| Cause | Documented storm/hail event | General aging, no specific event |
| Roof age | 15+ years with obsolete materials (full replacement) | Brand new roof with minor damage |
| Inspector | CW can do own inspection | Already signed with insurance-preferred contractor |
| Interior damage | Water stains, wall cracks (expands claim) | Roof-only with minimal damage |
| Prior work | None started | Already replaced roof |

### Roof-Specific Objections
- "Wear and tear" vs. storm damage (most common denial reason)
- Insurance claims damage predates current policy
- "Not enough damage to warrant replacement" — insurance wants to patch, not replace
- Prior roof work used as grounds for denial

## Fire Claims

**Distribution**: ~10% of all calls
**Common statuses**: Underpaid, New
**Note**: Fire claims are the highest-value claims. Average recovery potential is significantly higher than other types.

### Type-Specific Qualification Questions

**Extent of Damage**:
- "Was it a total loss, or partial?"
- "What areas of the home were damaged — structure, contents, or both?"
- "Was the house just a total loss?"
- "How far from the main house was the fire?" (for detached structure fires)

**Contents / Personal Property** (often the biggest part of fire claims):
- "How much personal property was lost?"
- "Have you started a contents list?"
- "Do you have photos of your belongings from before the fire?"
- "Did the insurance company give you a contents inventory?"
- "Did they price your items at Harbor Freight prices when they were Snap-On?" (common underpayment tactic)

**Displacement / ALE**:
- "Are you displaced?"
- "How much have you spent on temporary housing so far?"
- "Is insurance covering your additional living expenses?"
- "How long have you been out of the home?"

**Reports / Documentation**:
- "Do you have a fire marshal or fire department report?"
- "Was there a police report?"
- "What stage was the home in — any remodeling at the time?"
- "When did the fire happen?" (claim age check)

**Contractor / Rebuild**:
- "Do you have a contractor for the rebuild?"
- "Did you pick your own contractor, or did insurance send one?"
- "How much is the rebuild estimate?"

### Fire-Specific Red Flags

| Signal | Good Sign | Bad Sign |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| Total loss | Clear path to policy limits | Partial with ambiguous cause |
| Contents | Well-documented, high-value items | No photos, no receipts, nothing survived |
| Contractor | Independent, customer-chosen | Insurance-preferred vendor (quality/pricing concerns) |
| ALE | Customer displaced, expenses documented | Customer staying with family (less ALE to recover) |
| Claim age | Recent (within 6 months) | Over 1 year old |

### Fire-Specific Objections
- Insurance demanding receipts for items destroyed in the fire
- Preferred vendors doing poor quality work with cheap materials (laminate instead of hardwood)
- Insurance stopped payments citing failure to make mortgage payments
- Improper valuation — pricing Snap-On tools at Harbor Freight prices, aging items at 8-9 years when they're 1 year old

## Misc Claims (Vandalism, Theft, Business Income, Structural, Vehicle Impact)

**Distribution**: ~8% of all calls
**Common statuses**: Varies widely
**Note**: Most varied claim type — every call is unique. Reps need to qualify more heavily because the path isn't as well-worn.

### Type-Specific Qualification Questions

**Nature of Loss** (must identify first):
- "What caused the damage?"
- "Is this vandalism, theft, structural, or something else?"
- "Is this a residential or commercial property?"
- "How many units is the property?" (for commercial/multi-family)

**For Vandalism/Theft**:
- "Was there a police report filed?"
- "What specifically was taken or damaged?"
- "Do you have receipts or proof of ownership?"
- "Did the insurance company miscategorize the claim?" (common — e.g., "theft of money" vs. property damage)

**For Business Income Loss**:
- "How much did you do in revenue the year before the loss?"
- "What about the year after?"
- "Do you have tax returns for both years?"
- "Does the business income loss apply to all locations or just one?"

**For Structural/Vehicle Impact**:
- "Do you have an engineer's report?"
- "Is the other party's insurance involved?"
- "What percentage of the property was damaged?"

### Misc-Specific Red Flags

| Signal | Good Sign | Bad Sign |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| Documentation | Police report, receipts, engineer report | No proof, no receipts, no report |
| Claim type | Clear property damage, clear cause | Ambiguous, insurance can argue exclusions |
| Value | High-value contents, significant structural | Low-value items, minor damage |
| Insurance category | Correctly categorized | Miscategorized (common — fight the category first) |

### Misc-Specific Objections
- Insurance miscategorizing the claim type (theft of money vs. property damage)
- Conflicting engineer reports
- Business income loss requires extensive financial documentation
- Third-party liability disputes (neighbor's tree, vehicle impact)

## Key Questions That Separate Good Claims from Bad (All Types)

These are the most diagnostic questions across all claim types:

1. **"Did they put that in writing, or is it just verbal?"** — Verbal denials are easier to fight than written ones
2. **"How much do you think you need for all the repairs?"** — Claims under $20k are generally below CW's minimum
3. **"Have you completed repairs already?"** — Repaired = harder to inspect, but not a deal-killer if documented
4. **"Do you have photos of the damage?"** — No photos + no documentation = very difficult claim
5. **"Did you pick your own contractor, or did insurance send one?"** — Insurance-referred contractors produce biased estimates
6. **"When did this happen?"** — Claims older than 2 years may be outside statute of limitations
7. **"Do you have homeowner's insurance or renter's insurance?"** — Renters cannot file property claims (hard disqualifier)
8. **"Is the property under mortgage?"** — Mortgaged properties have more complex payout processes (not a disqualifier, but adds friction)
9. **"How's it been going with your insurance company so far?"** — Reveals status, frustration level, and what's already been tried
10. **"Did you report the damage as soon as you noticed it?"** — Delayed reporting = potential grounds for denial
