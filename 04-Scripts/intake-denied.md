# Script A: Denied Claim Intake

**Handed off to**: Low-Level Rep ($10-$12/hr)
**Goal**: Sign at 15%-20%
**Call type**: Inbound (customer found CW) or outbound (denied claims outreach)
**Average call length**: 8-15 minutes

---

## Who This Customer Is

Their insurance company has denied their claim. They've been told "no." They feel powerless, frustrated, and often desperate. Some have been fighting for months. Some just got the denial letter. A few have already tried lawyers or other adjusters.

Their emotional state drives this conversation more than any other factor. They need to believe someone can actually help — and they need to believe it fast, because they've been let down before.

---

## The Script

### Phase 1: Open (30 seconds)

**For inbound calls (customer called CW)**:
> "Hi, this is [Name] with Claim Warriors, the public adjusting office. This call will be recorded for quality purposes. How can I help you today?"

**For outbound calls (callback/website lead)**:
> "Hi, [Customer Name]. This is [Name] from Claim Warriors, the public adjusters office. You were on our website — do you have a home insurance claim you need help with?"

**For outbound calls (denied claims outreach)**:
> "Hi, [Customer Name]. This is [Name] from Claim Warriors. We're a public adjusting firm — we help homeowners fight denied insurance claims. I'm calling because we may be able to help with your situation. Do you have a minute?"

**What you're listening for**: Their tone. Frustrated? Skeptical? Desperate? Confused? This tells you which path the conversation takes.

---

### Phase 2: Let Them Tell Their Story (1-2 minutes)

**Ask one open question, then listen**:
> "Tell me what happened with your claim."

Or:
> "How's it been going with your insurance company so far?"

**DO**:
- Let them vent. Don't interrupt.
- Take notes on: claim type, insurance company, what happened, how long ago
- Listen for emotional cues — the more frustrated they are, the easier the close

**DON'T**:
- Rush to qualify
- Start explaining what CW does yet
- Interrupt their story

---

### Phase 3: Validate and Reframe (30 seconds)

After they finish, validate what they're feeling. Then reframe:

**If they're frustrated**:
> "Yeah, they do that to everyone. That's how they make money — they deny claims hoping people give up. Most people do. You're smart to not accept that."

**If they feel hopeless**:
> "I hear you. But just because they denied you doesn't mean it's over. The state gives public adjusters like us a license to fight denials. Insurance companies treat us differently because they know we have the legal standing to challenge them."

**If they're angry**:
> "You have every right to be upset. What they're doing is not right. That's exactly why firms like ours exist — to hold them accountable."

**Key line that works across all moods**:
> "A denial isn't the end. It's just the insurance company's first answer. Our job is to make them give a different answer."

---

### Phase 4: Quick Qualification (2-3 minutes)

Now qualify. Ask these in order. If any hard disqualifier hits, exit gracefully.

**Step 1 — Confirm basics**:
> "Are you the homeowner? Is your name on the insurance policy?"

*If renter → "Unfortunately, we can only work with policyholders — the person whose name is on the insurance policy. I'm sorry we can't help with this one."*

**Step 2 — Denial details**:
> "Did they send you a denial letter in writing, or was it just verbal?"

*Verbal denial = easier to fight. Written = harder but still possible.*

> "Do you know the reason they gave for denying it?"

*Common denial reasons: wear and tear, maintenance, pre-existing, policy exclusion, late reporting, fraud suspicion*

**Step 3 — Claim type** (branch to type-specific questions):
> "What kind of damage are we talking about — water, fire, roof, or something else?"

#### If Water:
> "What caused the water damage — pipe burst, leak, appliance, or something else?"
> "Is there mold?"
> "Did the insurance company say anything about 'gradual' or 'constant seepage'?"

#### If Roof:
> "What caused the roof damage — storm, hail, wind, or a tree?"
> "How old is your roof?"
> "Did they claim it was 'wear and tear'?"

#### If Fire:
> "Was it a total loss or partial?"
> "Are you displaced right now?"
> "Have you started a contents list?"

#### If Misc:
> "Can you describe the damage — vandalism, theft, structural, or something else?"
> "Was there a police report?"

**Step 4 — Value check**:
> "Do you have a sense of how much the repairs would cost? Even a rough number?"

*If clearly under $10k → "I want to be honest with you — our firm typically works claims that are $20,000 or more. For a claim this size, you might want to [suggest alternative]. I'm sorry I can't help more."*

**Step 5 — Timing**:
> "When did this happen?"

*If over 2 years → Check state-specific statute of limitations before proceeding.*

---

### Phase 5: Explain What CW Does (1-2 minutes)

Keep it simple. Three sentences max on what CW is, then pivot to what CW does *for them*.

> "So here's what we do. We're public adjusters — the state licenses us to represent homeowners against their insurance company. We're basically on your side of the fight."

> "What that means for you: we take over communication with your insurance company. We do our own inspection, write our own estimate, and negotiate for you. You don't have to talk to them anymore."

> "And here's the important part — we work on contingency. If we can't win you anything, you don't pay us a cent. Our fee is [15-20]% of what we recover for you, and only from what we get above what they've already offered."

**If they ask "how is that different from what I've already tried?"**:
> "When we submit our license to your insurance company, it changes the dynamic. They can't play the same games with us that they play with individual homeowners. We know the policy language, we know the tactics, and we have legal standing to challenge them."

---

### Phase 6: Handle Objections

Objections will come. The top ones for denied claims, in order of likelihood:

**"How do I know you're legit?"**
> "I totally understand — you've been burned already. Here's our license number: [provide it]. You can verify it with the state. And our fee structure protects you — if we don't win, you pay nothing."

**"I already tried a lawyer / another adjuster."**
> "I hear that a lot. Here's what's different about us — we're not litigation. We don't go to court. We negotiate directly with the insurance company using their own policy language. It's faster and you keep more of the money."

**"How much do you charge?"**
> "We charge [15-20]% of what we recover for you. Nothing upfront, nothing out of pocket. If we don't win, you don't pay. The percentage only applies to money we get you — not money you've already received."

**"I need to think about it" / "Let me talk to my spouse"**
> "Of course. I'll send you the information so you can review it. Can I call you back [specific day/time]? The sooner we get involved, the better — insurance companies use delay as a strategy."

**"Is it too late? My claim is old."**
> "When exactly did this happen? [Check timeline.] You're still within the window. But that's exactly why we should move quickly — the longer we wait, the less leverage we have."

---

### Phase 7: Close (1-2 minutes)

**Direct Ask** (most effective close for denied claims — 79% success rate):
> "I think we can help you with this. Let me get your information and send you the contract. What's your email address?"

Then collect:
1. Full name (as on policy)
2. Email address
3. Property address
4. Insurance company name
5. Policy number (if they have it)
6. Claim number (if they have it)
7. Date of loss

**Send the contract immediately while on the call**:
> "I just sent you the contract via DocuSign. Have you used DocuSign before? [If no] It's simple — you'll get an email, click the link, and follow the buttons to sign. Want me to walk you through it while we're on the phone?"

**If they hesitate at signing**:
> "Take your time reading it. The key thing to notice is paragraph [X] — it says if we don't win you anything, we don't charge you anything. That's the protection built in."

**After signing**:
> "Great. Here's what happens next: we're going to submit our license to your insurance company, which puts them on notice that you have professional representation. Then our adjuster will review your claim documents and schedule an inspection if needed. I'll be in touch within [timeframe] with an update."

---

### Phase 8: Wrap Up (30 seconds)

> "Do you have any other questions for me?"

> "If your insurance company calls you, don't answer. Let us handle all communication from here. If you need anything, call this number or text me directly."

> "We're going to fight this for you. That's what we do."

## Claim-Type Quick Reference (for during the call)

### Water — Denied
- Common denial reasons: "gradual leak," "constant seepage," "maintenance issue," "mold exclusion"
- CW angle: Challenge the "gradual" characterization. If pipe burst, it's sudden. Get plumber's report.
- Key question: "Was it a sudden event or did they claim it was gradual?"

### Roof — Denied
- Common denial reasons: "wear and tear," "age of roof," "pre-existing damage," "cosmetic only"
- CW angle: If roof is 15+ years and materials are discontinued, argue full replacement. Storm documentation.
- Key question: "Was there a specific storm or weather event, and how old is the roof?"

### Fire — Denied
- Common denial reasons: "arson suspicion," "policy lapsed," "vacancy clause," "underinsured"
- CW angle: Fire claims rarely get fully denied — more often severely underpaid. Focus on contents and ALE.
- Key question: "Did they deny the whole claim or just parts of it?"

### Misc — Denied
- Common denial reasons: "not a covered peril," "exclusion applies," "claim miscategorized"
- CW angle: Challenge the categorization. Insurance often puts claims in the wrong bucket to apply exclusions.
- Key question: "How did they categorize your claim, and does that match what actually happened?"
