# Script B: New or Underpaid Claim Intake

**Handed off to**: Sam / Account Manager ($25/hr)
**Goal**: Sign claim + perform sales valuation
**Call type**: Inbound or outbound
**Average call length**: 12-25 minutes (longer than denied — more assessment needed)

---

## Who This Customer Is

Two sub-types arrive here:

**New Claim Customer**: Just had damage. May or may not have filed with insurance yet. Doesn't know what to expect. Often confused, sometimes eager. They found CW proactively or were referred. They need guidance more than rescue.

**Underpaid Customer**: Filed a claim, got an offer from insurance, and it's way too low. They know something is wrong but don't know what to do about it. The gap between what insurance offered and what repairs actually cost is their pain point. They're frustrated but not as desperate as denied customers.

This is the more complex path. Sam needs to do two things the denied-claims rep doesn't:
1. **Assess the real value** of the claim (what is the gap between insurer offer and actual damage?)
2. **Perform a sales valuation** (what is CW's potential fee on this claim? Is it worth the firm's time?)

---

## The Script

### Phase 1: Open (30 seconds)

**For inbound calls**:
> "Hi, this is Sam with Claim Warriors. Thanks for calling — this call will be recorded for quality purposes. How can I help you today?"

**For outbound/callback**:
> "Hi, [Customer Name]. This is Sam Blaise with Claim Warriors. I think we've been playing some phone tag. [Or: You were on our website.] Do you have a home insurance claim I can help with?"

**For referral**:
> "Hi, [Customer Name]. This is Sam with Claim Warriors. [Referrer name] mentioned you're dealing with an insurance claim. I'd love to hear what's going on."

---

### Phase 2: Let Them Tell Their Story (2-3 minutes)

**Open with the power question**:
> "How's it been going with your insurance company so far?"

This one question does everything: it identifies the claim status, reveals the emotional state, and gets the customer talking about the gap between what they need and what they got.

**If they haven't filed yet (new claim)**:
> "Okay, so you haven't filed yet. That's actually a good position — it means we can get involved from the start and make sure your claim is filed correctly. Tell me about the damage."

**If they've filed and got an offer (underpaid)**:
> "Got it. So they offered you [amount]. And how much do you think the repairs will actually cost?"

**Listen for**:
- The gap (insurer offer vs. actual need) — this is your value proposition
- Claim type indicators (water, roof, fire, misc)
- How far along the process is
- Whether a contractor has been involved
- Emotional state and urgency

---

### Phase 3: Validate and Position (1 minute)

**For underpaid**:
> "Yeah, that's unfortunately very common. Insurance companies have a reputation for lowballing — they offer the minimum hoping you'll accept it. Most people don't realize they can fight for more. That's where we come in."

**For new claims**:
> "Good that you're looking into this now. The insurance company is going to send their own adjuster, and that adjuster works for them, not for you. Having us involved from the start means there's someone on your side making sure the claim is handled fairly."

**Key reframe for underpaid**:
> "The gap between what they offered and what you actually need — that's the money we fight for. And we usually win significantly more than the initial offer."

---

### Phase 4: Deep Qualification + Sales Valuation (5-8 minutes)

This is where Sam earns the $25/hr. You're not just qualifying — you're assessing the claim's potential value for CW.

**Step 1 — Basics**:
> "Let me get some details. Are you the homeowner? Your name is on the policy?"

> "What's your insurance company?"

> "What's the property address?"

**Step 2 — Claim type and cause** (branch here):
> "What kind of damage are we talking about?"

#### If Water:

> "What caused it — pipe burst, appliance, leak, sewer backup?"

*Sudden = covered. Gradual/seepage = harder.*

> "How many rooms were affected?"

> "Is there mold? Has anyone mentioned mold?"

> "Has remediation been done? Who did it?"

> "Are you displaced? How much have you spent on temporary housing?"

> "Do you have a plumber's report?"

**Sales valuation for water**: Typical contractor quotes range $30k-$100k. If insurance offered $7-20k, the gap is CW's playground. Add ALE if displaced. Mold adds complexity but also value. Multi-unit/HOA situations can involve multiple policies.

#### If Roof:

> "What caused the damage — storm, hail, wind, or a tree?"

> "How old is your roof?"

*15+ years = potential full replacement argument. Discontinued materials = gold.*

> "Has the insurance company sent someone to inspect?"

> "Did they say it was 'wear and tear' or acknowledge storm damage?"

> "Is there any interior damage — leaks, ceiling stains, wall cracks?"

> "Do you have a contractor estimate for the repairs?"

**Sales valuation for roof**: Contractor quotes for full replacement run $40-60k. Insurance often offers $2,800-$16,000. The "wear and tear" argument is the most common battleground. If the roof is old AND there was a storm, CW has a strong case for full replacement. Interior damage expands the claim significantly.

#### If Fire:

> "Was it a total loss or partial?"

> "Are you displaced right now? How long have you been out?"

> "How much have you spent on temporary housing so far?"

> "Have you started a contents list — an inventory of everything that was damaged or destroyed?"

> "Did you pick your own contractor for the rebuild, or did insurance send one?"

> "Do you have a fire department report?"

**Sales valuation for fire**: These are CW's highest-value claims. Total loss claims can hit policy limits ($170k-$500k+). Contents lists alone can be worth $50-200k. ALE adds $20-50k. If insurance offered $10-45k on a total loss, the recovery potential is massive. CW typically targets 3-10x the initial offer.

#### If Misc:

> "Can you walk me through exactly what happened?"

> "Is there a police report or engineer's report?"

> "Is this a residential or commercial property?"

> "How did the insurance company categorize the claim?"

**Sales valuation for misc**: Highly variable. Commercial properties and business income loss can be very high value. Vandalism/theft depends on documentation. Key is whether insurance miscategorized the claim — correcting the category alone can unlock coverage.

**Step 3 — The money question** (most important for sales valuation):
> "How much did they offer you so far?"

Then:
> "And how much do you think you actually need for all the repairs? Even a rough number."

Or if they don't know:
> "Do you have a contractor estimate? What's the contractor saying?"

**Calculate the gap mentally**: Contractor estimate minus insurance offer = the opportunity. CW typically negotiates to 2-3x contractor quotes. CW's fee is [20-25]% of the additional recovery.

**Example calculation**:
- Insurance offered: $15,000
- Contractor quotes: $50,000
- CW target: $100,000-$150,000 (2-3x contractor)
- Additional recovery: $85,000-$135,000
- CW fee (20%): $17,000-$27,000

*If the gap is too small (total claim under $20k), be honest*:
> "I want to be straight with you — at that claim size, our involvement might not make financial sense for either of us. [Suggest alternative or lower fee if borderline.]"

**Step 4 — Documentation and readiness**:
> "Do you have photos of the damage?"

> "Do you have a copy of your policy?"

> "Do you have the insurance company's estimate or offer letter?"

> "Have you already had contractors come out? Can you share those estimates with us?"

**Step 5 — Red flag check**:
> "Is the property under mortgage?"

*Not a dealbreaker — just means two-party checks.*

> "Have you worked with another public adjuster or attorney on this?"

*If yes, need to understand the situation before proceeding.*

> "Did you pick your own contractor, or did the insurance company recommend them?"

*Insurance-preferred contractors produce biased estimates.*

> "Have you completed any repairs already?"

*If yes, need photos from before repairs.*

---

### Phase 5: Present the Value Proposition (2-3 minutes)

This is where Sam connects the dots. You've gathered the data — now show them the math.

**For underpaid — the gap close**:
> "So here's the picture. Your insurance offered you $[X]. Your contractor is saying $[Y]. That gap is exactly what we fight for."

> "But here's what most people don't know — our estimates are typically 2-3 times higher than what contractors quote. We use industry-standard pricing software and we account for every line item that the insurance company conveniently left out."

> "So realistically, we're going to push for something in the range of $[2-3x contractor quote]. Even after our fee of [20-25]%, you come out way ahead of where you'd be negotiating on your own."

**For new claims — the prevention close**:
> "What we do is get involved from the start. We submit our license to your insurance company, which means they know you have professional representation. That changes how they handle your claim."

> "We'll do our own inspection, write our own estimate, and negotiate with them on every line item. The insurance company's adjuster works for them — we work for you."

> "Our estimates are usually significantly higher than what insurance companies initially offer. Most customers who deal with insurance directly leave tens of thousands of dollars on the table."

**Always include the fee structure**:
> "We charge [20-25]% — and only on what we recover for you. Nothing out of pocket, nothing upfront. If we don't win you anything, you pay us nothing."

**If they ask about the percentage**:
> "That's our standard rate. Keep in mind — without us, you'd be accepting $[insurer offer]. With us, we're targeting $[CW target]. Even after our fee, you're getting significantly more than you would on your own."

*For large claims, Sam has authority to negotiate the percentage down*:
> "Since this is a larger claim, I can work with you at 20% instead of 25%."

---

### Phase 6: Handle Objections

**"How much do you charge?"** (most common for new/underpaid)
> "We charge [20-25]% of what we win you. Nothing upfront. And the percentage only applies to money we get above what they've already offered. So if they offered $15,000 and we get them to $80,000, our fee applies to the $65,000 difference."

**"My insurance said that's all they'll pay."**
> "That's what they say to everyone. The insurance company's job is to pay as little as possible. Our job is to show them the claim is worth more. We do this by reviewing your policy, doing our own inspection, and writing a detailed estimate they can't ignore."

**"I already have a contractor estimate."**
> "Great — that actually helps us. We'll use your contractor's estimate as a baseline, but our estimates typically come in 2-3 times higher because we account for line items contractors don't — overhead, profit, code upgrades, hidden damage, depreciation recovery."

**"But if I file against my insurance, won't they drop me or raise my rates?"**
> "That's a possibility. But here's the thing — you're paying premiums for this exact situation. Your policy exists to cover this damage. And the difference between what they're offering and what you're owed could be $50,000 or more. That far outweighs any premium increase."

**"I'm not in a huge hurry."**
> "I understand. But the insurance company benefits from delay — the longer you wait, the harder it gets. Evidence degrades, witnesses forget, and they use that against you. Getting started now, even if the timeline is longer, protects your position."

**"What if they offered me a decent amount and you can't get more?"**
> "Then you don't pay us anything. That's the beauty of contingency — we're motivated to get you the most possible because that's how we get paid too."

---

### Phase 7: Close (2-3 minutes)

**The value-based close** (most effective for new/underpaid):
> "Based on everything you've told me, I think there's a strong opportunity here. Your insurance offered $[X], your contractor is quoting $[Y], and based on claims like yours, we typically recover $[Z range]. I'd like to get started — let me send you the contract."

Collect:
1. Full name (as on policy)
2. Spouse/co-insured name (if applicable)
3. Email address
4. Property address
5. Insurance company name
6. Policy number
7. Claim number
8. Date of loss
9. Best phone number

**Send contract while on the call**:
> "I just sent it to your email via DocuSign. It should be there now — want to check?"

**Walk them through it**:
> "When you open it, you'll see a 'Review Document' button. Click through — the key section is [paragraph about fees]. It confirms the [20-25]% fee applies only to what we win you, and if we don't win anything, you owe nothing."

> "There are yellow tabs where you need to initial and sign. Just follow the tabs and click 'Finish' at the end."

**After signing**:
> "Here's what happens next:"
> "1. We submit our license to [Insurance Company]. This puts them on notice."
> "2. Our adjuster reviews your claim documents and policy."
> "3. We schedule an inspection of your property — we'll coordinate a time that works for you."
> "4. We write our own estimate using industry-standard pricing."
> "5. We negotiate with the insurance company on every line item."
> "I'll call you within [48 hours / 1 week] with an update after we've reviewed everything."

---

### Phase 8: Post-Close Data Collection (2-3 minutes)

Sam needs to capture information for the back office. If not already collected:

> "Before we go — a few more things that will help us get started faster."

- Mortgage company name (if applicable)
- Contractor name and contact (if they have one)
- Any photos they can send (text or email)
- Any documents — policy, estimates, denial letters, inspection reports
- Available dates for CW inspection

> "Can you send me whatever you have — photos, estimates, the insurance company's letter — to [email]? The more we have upfront, the faster we move."

---

### Phase 9: Wrap Up (30 seconds)

> "Do you have any other questions?"

> "One important thing: if the insurance company contacts you from now on, don't engage. Let us handle all communication. If they call, you can just say 'talk to my public adjuster' and give them our number."

> "I'm going to take care of this for you. That's what we do."

| $10,000 | $15,000 | $30-45k | $4-7k | Borderline |

|----------------|-----------------|-----------|--------------|-----------|

### Misc Claims
| Insurance Offer | Contractor Quote | CW Target | CW Fee (20%) | Worth It? |
|----------------|-----------------|-----------|--------------|-----------|

**Rule of thumb**: If the gap between insurance offer and contractor quote is under $10k, the claim is probably too small. If it's over $20k, it's a clear yes.
